// The first story names the failure. The second explains the system.

The operator is visible; the system is usually harder to see.

A habit of mine is watching Air Crash Investigation and then reading the accident reports behind the episodes — not for the drama, but for the systems thinking.

I find myself reading them the way an engineer might read a production post-mortem: slowly, looking past the immediate failure and asking what had to be true in the wider system for that failure to become possible.

The pattern is rarely simple. A pilot, controller, mechanic, dispatcher, or maintainer may appear at the sharp end of the incident. But beneath that moment there is often a quieter set of conditions: an alarm that had become too noisy to trust, a sensor feeding bad information into automation, a deferred fix, a procedure that worked in theory but not under pressure, or a test environment that never really resembled the real world.

What interests me is not blame as an endpoint, but the recurring conditions — technical, organisational, and political — that make failure possible.

Below are 32 cases, cross-referenced against their official reports, and read through two lenses: the proximate explanation that made the event visible, and the deeper organisational, technical, and operational conditions that allowed it to unfold.

The aim is not to draw easy analogies between aviation and software, but to ask a more useful question: what failure modes keep reappearing in complex systems, whether or not they have wings?

PROX — what was blamed
The probable cause as written. Usually the last human to touch it.
ROOT — what was true
The latent condition beneath it — in the design, the data, or the process.
POWER — what was left out
Where the report fell short: cost, influence, or who got to write it.
THE DEEPEST PATTERN ISN'T IN THE COCKPIT

Read enough of these cases and the strongest pattern is not only technical, but systemic — shaped by incentives, oversight, commercial pressure, and decisions made far upstream of the failure.

When the fuller truth surfaces, it is often forced into view by someone without a stake in preserving the original story: a grieving parent, a dissenting investigator, a whistleblower, a lawsuit, or a public inquiry.

That is the lesson mature engineering cultures eventually learn. A post-mortem is useful only when it can examine the conditions around the failure, not merely the person nearest to it. Otherwise it becomes punishment theatre, and the latent fault stays exactly where it was.

FAILURE MODES · transferable9 patterns

The same shapes recur across the ledger. Each is stated once for aviation, once for any complex system — the analogy is the whole point.

CRITICALMonitoring & alerting — five ways the channel breaks

The link between the machine's state and the human's grasp of it fails in five distinct ways — only the last is alert fatigue:

Can't read it — a display that reads plausibly normal while it lies, or a dial easy to misread; nothing flags the error. · Alitalia 404
Mixed signals — alarms that contradict each other: overspeed and stall at the same instant, three airspeeds all disagreeing. · Birgenair 301 · Aeroperú 603
Too much of it — a cascade of alarms floods the crew past what they can absorb. · Qantas 32 · Qantas 72
Didn't fire — the warning that should have caught the slip was silently dead, or came too late. · Delta 1141 · Ansett 703
Cried wolf — false alarms train the crew to ignore it, or pull its breaker. · Trigana 267 · Linate 686

SAME SHAPE → A monitor is only as good as the operator's ability to read it, in time, and trust it. Ambiguity, contradiction, overload, silent failure, and false alarms each defeat it differently — so the answer is rarely "add more alerts."

CRITICALAutomation authority — who wins the disagreement

When the machine and the human gave opposite orders, nothing said who wins. One jet's collision system said climb while the controller said descend; a flight computer pushed the nose down on data the pilots knew was false and could not instantly countermand.

SAME SHAPE → Shared control needs a defined precedence and a graceful override. High-authority automation must surface what it is doing and why, and stand down the moment its inputs are suspect.

STEP BACK · RISK DOESN'T VANISH — IT MOVES, COMPOUNDS, OR MULTIPLIES

The next two failure modes — plus a third move scattered across the ledger — are one idea in three guises. Skip the work of pricing risk up front and it doesn't vanish — it moves, compounds, or is minted fresh by the feature you added. Three forms:

TRANSFER →A weak design — or a part that can be mis-set or mis-calibrated — hands the risk to the people downstream: every install, inspection, and pre-flight checkout must be perfect, forever. · no fail-safe · BA 5390 · Gimli 143 · Tuninter 1153
ACCUMULATE →Deferring known work lets the risk compound over time, like interest you can't see on the books. · deferred reliability · Alaska 261
INTRODUCE →A new feature, added to make the product desirable, brings fresh risk surface that the appeal hides — the seat-back entertainment was the draw; its wiring was the fire. · Swissair 111

Propping up an inferior design with maintenance feels like prudence; usually it is transfer and accumulation at once, billed later to someone else. The only real exit is the feedback loop — log what maintenance keeps catching and bake it into the next design — while remembering to treat every new feature as risk surface, not just a selling point.

WARNINGNo fail-safe — a design that mortgages safety to a routine task

A windscreen bolted on from outside, so only the fasteners hold it against cabin pressure; a cargo-door latch that reads "locked" without being locked, its weakness known yet left unfixed for years; a single jackscrew nut with no backup. The design carried no margin — so one ordinary slip downstream was enough.

SAME SHAPE → A design with no fail-safe silently demands that every install and inspection downstream be perfect forever. Build the margin in — redundancy, a guard that rejects the wrong part or state, a way to fail safe — rather than mortgaging safety to flawless execution.

BA 5390United 811Turkish 981Alaska 261Propair 420
WARNINGDeferred reliability is a loan — it compounds

An inspection interval stretched four-fold; a flammable material kept in the fleet because retrofit was costly; a mismatched part forced in to make a schedule; one lone, unverified hand on a safety-critical task.

SAME SHAPE → Maintenance debt accrues silently and off the balance sheet. The interest comes due all at once, at the worst possible moment, and rarely on the watch of whoever deferred it.

Descending below minimums without the runway in sight; pressing an approach into a storm; landing past the crosswind limit — risks run so often they stopped feeling like risks, until a low-margin day made the bill payable.

SAME SHAPE → When a shortcut keeps working it quietly becomes the procedure. "Press on" feels rational as the margins erode — the deviation is invisible right up until it is the headline.

BASELINEFixation — the whole team watching the wrong dial

An entire crew absorbed by a burnt-out indicator bulb, or a hung landing gear, while — unwatched — the aircraft flew gently into the ground. The step that got skipped was the load-bearing one.

SAME SHAPE → When every operator converges on the same low-stakes anomaly, nobody guards the primary objective. Keep at least one set of eyes on the thing that actually kills you.

FOLLOW THE MONEY · COMMERCIAL PRESSURE

Behind a striking share of these sits one upstream force that rarely makes the probable cause: the cost line. It stretches the maintenance interval, fits the cheaper part, flies the schedule the weather warned against — then lets the operator take the fall. Nearly half of the cases below carry it, tagged commercial, in five forms:

Cut the maintenance — stretch the inspection interval, skip the independent check, thin the night shift, run a low-cost flag-of-convenience operator. · Alaska 261 · Air Midwest 5481 · Aeroperú 603 · Birgenair 301 · BA 5390 · Continental 2574
Fit the cheaper part — an older or mismatched component, slotted in to save money. · Air Transat 236
Fly the schedule — get-there-itis: the on-time number outranks the weather margin. · American 1420 · TransAsia 222
Grow past your competence — expand or chase rivals faster than training and SOPsStandard Operating Procedures can carry; keep a marginal pilot flying because you're short. · TransAsia 235 · TransAsia 222 · Crossair 3597
Sell the sizzle, bury the fix — ship the feature that sells; leave the unglamorous safety change for later, or quietly. · Swissair 111 · United 811 · Turkish 981 · Propair 420

It is the textbook "contributing factor" — real enough to bend every decision, diffuse enough that no one signs for it. But it is tempting to file capitalism itself as the cause, and that is too easy. Commercial aviation runs on risk the airline and the passenger both agree to carry — the only real question is how much, and at what price. Safety advances when the technology to raise the bar — better testing, the R&D behind a new approach — finally falls cheap enough to adopt. Until then a business under cost pressure survives by carrying the risk a little longer, and a regulator, lobbied on what the fix would cost, sometimes lets it. The cost line isn't the villain of these stories so much as the boundary condition the report rarely names: the price at which "safe enough" got set.

GET-THERE-ITIS · THE JUDGMENT NO SCHEDULE CAN MAKE FOR YOU

Aviation already has a name for the strongest of these pulls: get-there-itis — the destination quietly outranking the margins. Its antidote isn't another procedure; it is the airmanship to stop the revenue and take the cost before the cost takes you. Knowing when to break off, divert, or refuse the approach is one of the hardest and least teachable skills there is — less a procedure than a judgment call about what risk is worth carrying, and for whom. The best operators are defined less by what they can push through than by what they choose not to.

ROOTThey said "priority" and "running out of fuel" — never the word "emergency" that triggers action. No standard low-fuel phraseology.
POWERA safety net that hinged on one magic word and a non-native crew's assertiveness; controllers weren't obliged to probe the ambiguity. A simple fuel-state protocol — Pan-PanUrgency call — serious, one level below a Mayday at one threshold, MaydayDistress call — grave and imminent danger at the next — would have replaced the need to be assertive with a rule that fires on its own.
